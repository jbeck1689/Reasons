# Content Framework: Buddhist Studies & Critical Thinking Branch

## Working Name Options

- **See For Yourself** — Ehipassiko translated as brand. Direct,
  invitational, no mysticism. Risk: vague without context.
- **The Untangling** — References the Pāli "jatā" (tangle), used by
  the Buddha to describe the human condition. Risk: obscure.
- **Clear Seeing** — Maps to vipassanā without using the Pāli.
  Clean, active. Risk: sounds like an optometrist.
- **The Four Tasks** — Borrows Batchelor's reframing directly.
  Signals the practical, non-doctrinal approach. Risk: too insider.
- **What Is This?** — The Seon/Zen inquiry question. Immediate,
  disarming. Risk: already a Batchelor book title.

No need to commit now. Notice which ones you react to.

## Core Design Principle

This branch shares the same foundational principle as the existing
platform: the learner is never a student being catechized. The
learner is someone whose own mind is running patterns — patterns
that cause unnecessary suffering — and their job is to catch those
patterns in action.

The adversarial agent is not an external manipulator (salesperson,
politician) as in the reasoning courses. The adversarial agent is
the learner's own untrained cognition: craving, aversion, and
delusion operating below the threshold of awareness.

The four noble truths provide the structural framework — not as
articles of faith to be accepted, but as a diagnostic model to be
tested against direct experience. The Buddha himself framed them
this way. In the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta, each truth carries
an injunction: suffering is to be understood, its origin is to be
abandoned, its cessation is to be realized, the path is to be
developed. These are tasks, not beliefs.

Feedback language follows the same adversarial pattern as the
existing courses:
- "You caught it." / "Your mind slipped past you. Here's what
  happened."
- "That's the pattern running." / "You saw through the story."

---

## Audience & Scope

This branch is built for one person first. The audience is an
adult with:
- Existing familiarity with the concept of cognitive bias and
  informal fallacy (i.e., has completed or could complete the
  existing reasoning courses)
- Personal experience with suffering — not as an abstract concept
  but as lived reality, including but not limited to trauma
- No prior Buddhist education required, but intellectual seriousness
  assumed — the content does not condescend or oversimplify
- Interest in why these patterns exist (mechanism), not just that
  they exist (doctrine)

Scale is not a design goal. Quality and personal utility are.

## Interpretive Position

This framework makes specific interpretive choices that should be
stated explicitly rather than passed off as neutral.

### What we draw from

- **Early sutta material** (Pāli Nikāyas) as primary source, using
  Bhikkhu Bodhi's translations as the textual baseline.
- **Batchelor's reframing** of the four noble truths as four tasks
  (ELSA: Embrace, Let go, See it stop, Act) — not because it is
  the "correct" reading, but because it is the most operationally
  useful for a learning platform built around pattern recognition
  and skill development.
- **Cognitive neuroscience** (Davidson, van der Kolk, Siegel) as a
  parallel lens — not to validate Buddhism, but to show convergence
  where it exists and divergence where it doesn't.
- **Trauma-informed awareness** (Treleaven) as a design constraint,
  not a content focus. The platform should never inadvertently push
  a learner into dysregulation.

### What we set aside

- Rebirth, karma as metaphysical doctrine, cosmology, and any claim
  requiring faith rather than verification through experience.
- Commentarial tradition (Abhidhamma, Visuddhimagga) except where
  it illuminates an early sutta concept.
- Sectarian claims about which tradition "got it right."
- The impulse to prove Buddhism is "scientifically validated." The
  content presents convergences honestly and leaves the reader to
  draw conclusions.

### What we acknowledge

- This is one interpretation among many. Traditional Theravāda,
  Mahāyāna, and Vajrayāna practitioners would read much of this
  material differently. That is noted where relevant, without
  apology and without dismissal.
- Batchelor's secular reading is contested. Bhikkhu Anālayo, among
  others, has argued it strips essential elements. The learner
  should know this debate exists.

## Course Structure

The four noble truths organize the branch into four courses.
Each course contains 6 sequences. Each sequence is 8 steps,
following the existing rhythm:

INSTRUCTION → INSTRUCTION → MULTIPLE_CHOICE → INSTRUCTION →
MULTIPLE_CHOICE → INSTRUCTION → FREE_RESPONSE → INSTRUCTION

This gives 4 courses × 6 sequences × 8 steps = 192 steps total.

---

### Course 1: Something Is Off
*The First Truth — Dukkha: Understanding Suffering*

The entry point. Teaches the learner to recognize dukkha not as
a grand philosophical claim about existence but as something
happening right now, in identifiable patterns, with identifiable
textures. Dukkha is the gap between how things are and how your
mind insists they should be.

**Tone:** Direct, warm, grounded in everyday experience. No Pāli
terms without immediate English rendering. Use scenarios from
ordinary life — work frustration, relationship conflict, the
3 AM anxiety loop — not monastery settings.

**Neuroscience garnish:** The default mode network and its role in
rumination. Davidson's research on how the untrained mind defaults
to self-referential processing that amplifies dissatisfaction.
Van der Kolk's observation that the body registers suffering before
the conscious mind names it — dukkha as felt sense, not concept.

Sequences:
1. **The Gap** — Dukkha as the distance between "what is" and "what
   I want." Not that life is suffering, but that resistance to
   life-as-it-is generates a specific kind of friction.
2. **Three Flavors** — The three characteristics of dukkha:
   ordinary suffering (dukkha-dukkha), suffering of change
   (viparinama-dukkha), and the pervasive unsatisfactoriness of
   conditioned experience (sankhara-dukkha). Each made concrete
   through scenario.
3. **The Body Knows First** — Vedanā (feeling-tone) as the first
   signal. Pleasant, unpleasant, neutral — the bare registration
   before the story starts. Where neuroscience on interoception
   and the Buddha's emphasis on bodily awareness converge.
4. **The Stories We Tell** — How raw sensation becomes narrative.
   The move from "my chest is tight" to "my boss doesn't respect
   me" to "I should quit" — a chain of fabrication that happens
   in milliseconds.
5. **Nothing Stays** — Anicca (impermanence) as observable fact,
   not metaphysical claim. The learner practices noticing that
   mental states, emotions, and even physical sensations are
   constantly changing — and that suffering intensifies precisely
   when we grip what's moving.
6. **Who's Suffering?** — Anattā (non-self) introduced carefully.
   Not "you don't exist" but "the fixed self you're defending
   may be a construction." The modular model of mind from
   cognitive science (Minsky, Kurzban, Wright) as a parallel lens.

### Course 2: The Engine
*The Second Truth — Samudaya: The Origin of Suffering*

Teaches the learner to identify the mechanism — craving (tanhā)
and its three modes: craving for pleasure, craving for becoming,
craving for non-existence. This is where the adversarial frame
hits hardest: the learner's own craving is the con artist.

**Tone:** Slightly more confrontational. "Your mind just did it
again" energy. The learner should feel caught, not lectured.

**Neuroscience garnish:** The dopamine reward prediction system
and how it generates wanting that outlasts satisfaction.
Davidson's research on how reactivity patterns become neural
habits. The hedonic treadmill described in both Buddhist and
evolutionary psychology terms.

Sequences:
1. **The Want Machine** — Tanhā as the engine. Not desire itself
   but the compulsive, automatic quality of craving — the
   difference between choosing to eat and being driven to eat.
   Wright's framing of natural selection as having "designed"
   craving to be insatiable.
2. **Chasing Pleasant** — Kāma-tanhā: craving for sensory
   pleasure. The pattern of acquisition → brief satisfaction →
   renewed craving. Scenarios: the scroll, the purchase, the
   next bite.
3. **Becoming Someone** — Bhava-tanhā: craving to be. The drive
   to construct and maintain a self-image, a status, an identity.
   The suffering embedded in "I need to be seen as..."
4. **Making It Stop** — Vibhava-tanhā: craving for non-existence.
   Aversion, avoidance, the desire to not-feel. Where trauma
   literature converges: van der Kolk's description of numbing
   and dissociation as the body's attempt to annihilate unbearable
   experience. Treleaven's caution that meditative practices can
   inadvertently trigger this mode.
5. **The Chain** — Paticca-samuppāda (dependent origination) in
   simplified form: contact → feeling → craving → clinging →
   becoming. Not the full twelve-link chain but the operationally
   relevant segment. The learner traces this chain in their own
   recent experience.
6. **It's Not Your Fault (But It Is Your Problem)** — The
   evolutionary and developmental roots of reactivity. Natural
   selection built the craving engine. Early experience may have
   calibrated the sensitivity. Understanding the origin is not
   about blame — it's about seeing the mechanism clearly enough
   to interrupt it.

### Course 3: The Clearing
*The Third Truth — Nirodha: The Cessation of Suffering*

Teaches the learner to recognize moments when craving has stopped
— not as a permanent state to be achieved but as something that
happens naturally and can be noticed. Batchelor's reframing is
most useful here: nirodha not as a distant goal but as the source
of the path, the turning point available in any moment.

**Tone:** Quieter. More spacious. Less adversarial, because the
content is about what's already there when reactivity drops away.

**Neuroscience garnish:** Davidson's research on long-term
meditators showing reduced amygdala reactivity and increased
prefrontal regulation. The neuroscience of the "relaxation
response" vs. the deeper claim of altered traits — being honest
about what the evidence supports and where it's still emerging.
Siegel's "window of tolerance" as a practical framework for
understanding regulated vs. dysregulated states.

Sequences:
1. **Already Happening** — Moments of non-craving in ordinary
   life. The pause between activities. The instant after a laugh
   before the next thought. Nirodha is not exotic — it's the
   background that craving covers up.
2. **What Reactivity Feels Like When It Stops** — Training
   attention on the absence rather than the presence. Most people
   have never been asked to notice what's there when suffering
   isn't.
3. **The Space Between** — The gap between stimulus and response.
   Viktor Frankl's observation reframed in Buddhist terms: between
   contact and craving, there is a moment. The entire path lives
   in that moment.
4. **Not What You Think** — Common misunderstandings of nirodha:
   it's not bliss, not numbness, not dissociation, not permanent.
   Directly addressing the risk that "cessation" gets confused
   with "checking out." Treleaven's framework on distinguishing
   healthy equanimity from trauma-driven dissociation.
5. **Letting Go vs. Pushing Away** — The critical difference
   between genuine release and suppression. Aversion to craving
   is still craving. The learner practices recognizing the
   difference in their own experience.
6. **Trust the Process** — Ehipassiko operationalized: the
   invitation to verify through direct experience, not belief.
   The learner reflects on what they've actually noticed so far
   vs. what they've merely intellectually accepted.

### Course 4: The Practice
*The Fourth Truth — Magga: The Path*

Teaches the eightfold path not as eight separate rules but as an
integrated way of operating — a protocol for living with less
unnecessary suffering. Batchelor's framing: the path is what opens
up when reactivity stops. It's not something you do to achieve
cessation; it's what becomes possible because of cessation.

**Tone:** Practical, concrete, action-oriented. "Here's what this
looks like on a Tuesday morning."

**Neuroscience garnish:** Davidson and Goleman's distinction
between state effects (feeling calm after meditating) and trait
effects (being measurably less reactive six months later). The
evidence for neuroplasticity as the mechanism by which practice
reshapes default patterns. Keeping honest about what's
well-established vs. what's preliminary.

Sequences:
1. **Seeing Clearly** — Sammā-diṭṭhi (right view) and
   sammā-saṅkappa (right intention). Not "correct beliefs" but
   "seeing what's actually happening" and "orienting toward
   non-harm." How the reasoning skills from the existing courses
   connect directly: clear thinking is right view in action.
2. **Speaking and Acting** — Sammā-vācā (right speech),
   sammā-kammanta (right action), sammā-ājīva (right livelihood).
   Ethics as practical engineering, not moralism. "When you speak
   with less reactivity, your relationships change. That's not a
   spiritual claim. It's a mechanical one."
3. **Training Attention** — Sammā-vāyāma (right effort),
   sammā-sati (right mindfulness), sammā-samādhi (right
   concentration). The meditation-adjacent skills: effort as
   balanced engagement, mindfulness as nonreactive awareness,
   concentration as the capacity to stay with what's difficult.
4. **When Practice Meets Resistance** — What happens when the
   path gets hard. Doubt, restlessness, boredom, the desire to
   quit. The five hindrances as a practical troubleshooting guide,
   not a moral failing.
5. **Integration** — How the eight factors work together, not
   sequentially. A realistic look at what "practice" means when
   you're not on a meditation cushion — in traffic, in a
   disagreement, at 2 AM with your thoughts.
6. **The Long Game** — Honest accounting of what this path
   offers and what it doesn't. It doesn't eliminate pain. It
   doesn't make you special. It reduces the unnecessary suffering
   you add to unavoidable difficulty. That's enough.

## Step Design Patterns (Branch-Specific)

These extend the existing patterns from content-framework.md with
Buddhism-specific adaptations.

### Pattern: The Mirror (replaces The Setup)
Type: INSTRUCTION
A short scenario (3–5 sentences) drawn from ordinary life that
demonstrates a specific form of dukkha or craving in action.
Written in second person. The key difference from the reasoning
courses: the adversarial agent is the learner's own mind, not
an external actor.

Example: "You check your phone. Nothing new. You put it down.
Thirty seconds later, you check it again. What just happened
wasn't a decision. It was a pull — automatic, unexamined, and
slightly unsatisfying even as you obeyed it."

### Pattern: The Catch (adapted)
Type: MULTIPLE_CHOICE
Same structure as existing: a scenario where a specific mental
pattern is operating. The learner identifies which form of
craving, aversion, or delusion is at work. Wrong answers are
plausible misidentifications with teaching explanations.

### Pattern: The Reversal
Type: MULTIPLE_CHOICE (designed to fool the learner)
Parallel to "The Trap" in existing courses. The learner is
presented with a situation that looks like wisdom but is actually
a subtle form of craving or aversion. Example: spiritual
bypassing framed as equanimity. If they fall for it, feedback
says: "That felt like letting go. It was actually pushing away.
Here's the difference."

### Pattern: The Trace
Type: FREE_RESPONSE
The learner traces a chain of dependent origination in their own
recent experience: a moment of contact → feeling → craving →
clinging → suffering. This is the externalization principle at
work — understanding becomes real when articulated.

### Pattern: The Convergence
Type: INSTRUCTION
A concept from the sutta material is placed alongside a finding
from cognitive neuroscience or clinical psychology. Neither is
presented as validating the other. The learner sees two lenses
focused on the same phenomenon and draws their own conclusions.

### Pattern: The Sutta Window
Type: INSTRUCTION
A short passage from the Pāli canon (Bhikkhu Bodhi translation)
is presented with plain-language context. Not a translation
exercise — a moment of direct contact with the source material.
The learner sees where the ideas they've been working with
actually come from.

## Trauma-Informed Design Constraints

These are not content topics. They are constraints on how all
content in this branch is built.

1. **No instructions to "sit with" intense negative emotion
   without providing an exit.** Every practice-adjacent prompt
   includes an explicit option to redirect attention outward or
   to stop.

2. **Distinguish equanimity from dissociation.** When teaching
   nirodha or non-reactivity, explicitly name the difference
   between "I'm present and not gripped by this" and "I've
   checked out." Treleaven's window of tolerance framework
   informs this.

3. **No shame in reactivity.** The adversarial framing ("your
   mind tricked you") must never slide into "you failed." The
   feedback language acknowledges that reactivity is the default,
   biologically and developmentally. Catching it is the skill.
   Not catching it is the starting point, not a moral failure.

4. **Craving for non-existence (vibhava-tanhā) handled with
   care.** This sequence directly touches on avoidance, numbing,
   and the desire to disappear — experiences that are clinically
   significant for trauma survivors. Content is informational and
   observational, never prescriptive. No suggestion that
   meditation is a substitute for professional support.

5. **Body-based prompts are always invitational.** "Notice if
   there's any sensation in your chest" — not "Feel what's in
   your body." The conditional phrasing preserves agency.

## Pāli Study Module (Separate Track, Future)

Not part of the four-course critical thinking structure, but
architecturally planned.

- Uses INSTRUCTION, FILL_IN_BLANK, MATCHING, and FREE_RESPONSE
  step types already in the platform schema.
- Organized by sutta, not by grammatical concept.
- Each sequence takes one short sutta or sutta excerpt, provides
  Bhikkhu Bodhi's translation alongside the Pāli, and walks
  through key terms and their semantic range.
- The externalization feature: the learner produces their own
  rendering of a key passage and articulates why they made
  specific translation choices.
- Not a Pāli grammar course. A "reading with assistance" course
  that builds vocabulary and interpretive confidence over time.

## Source Attribution & Integrity

Every claim in the content that references a specific author or
researcher must be traceable. The content should function as if
the learner could, at any point, ask "where does this come from?"
and get a clear answer.

- **Sutta references:** Nikāya, sutta number, and Bhikkhu Bodhi
  translation page where applicable.
- **Neuroscience claims:** Author, study or book, and an honest
  statement of evidence strength (well-established / emerging /
  single-study).
- **Interpretive choices:** When following Batchelor's reading
  over a traditional one, say so. When the platform makes a
  pedagogical simplification, say so.

The goal is intellectual honesty as a design value, not footnotes
as decoration.

## What This Branch Is Not

- It is not a meditation instruction app. It teaches about the
  mind's patterns; it does not guide meditation practice.
- It is not a substitute for a teacher or a sangha. It is a
  self-study tool for one person.
- It is not a claim that Buddhism is "the best" framework for
  understanding suffering. It is an investigation of one
  framework, taken seriously on its own terms and cross-examined
  with contemporary tools.
- It is not therapy. Where content touches on clinically
  significant experiences (trauma, dissociation, persistent
  distress), it points toward professional support, not inward
  toward more self-study.

## Voice & Style Guide

The prose in this branch should sound like:

- **Batchelor's intellectual clarity** — precise, unhurried,
  treating the reader as capable of handling complexity.
- **Wright's conversational warmth** — accessible, occasionally
  self-deprecating, willing to say "this is hard to explain."
- **Van der Kolk's clinical groundedness** — always connecting
  the abstract to the embodied, always asking "what does this
  feel like from the inside?"
- **None of their weaknesses.** Not Batchelor's occasional
  emotional distance. Not Wright's tendency to over-explain.
  Not van der Kolk's occasional overreach beyond the evidence.

The voice is direct, warm, precise, and never pious. It sounds
like someone who has read the suttas carefully and also knows
what it feels like to be awake at 3 AM with their own craving
running the show.

### Specific stylistic rules:

- No Pāli term without an immediate English rendering on first
  use. After that, the Pāli can stand alone.
- No appeals to tradition or authority. "The Buddha said" is not
  an argument. "The Buddha described a pattern — here it is in
  your own experience" is the move.
- Neuroscience references are garnish, not main course. They
  appear 1-2 times per sequence, not in every step.
- Blockquotes from the suttas (Bhikkhu Bodhi translations) appear
  in "The Sutta Window" pattern steps, not scattered throughout.
- The word "spiritual" does not appear. The word "practice"
  refers to pattern recognition skill development, not
  devotional activity.
